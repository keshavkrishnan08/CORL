% Compounding-error account of the validation gap. Correct, tight, and tied to the
% experiments: it predicts H2's functional form and grounds the coherence hypothesis.

Validation loss and deployment success diverge for a reason that can be stated precisely. A
short bound explains both halves of our study: why the gap grows with horizon and contact (the
regime dependence H2 tests), and why metrics of accumulated deviation predict success where
per-step error does not (the coherence hypothesis).

\paragraph{Setup.} Let the dynamics be $s_{t+1}=f(s_t,a_t)$ with $f$ Lipschitz,
$\|f(s,a)-f(s',a')\|\le L_s\|s-s'\|+L_a\|a-a'\|$. Let $\pi^\star$ be the expert and
$g(s):=f(s,\pi^\star(s))$ the expert closed-loop map, with Lipschitz constant $L:=\mathrm{Lip}(g)$;
$L$ captures how strongly the task amplifies or absorbs disturbances, and contact events make it
large. A learned policy $\hat\pi$ has per-step action error $\|\hat\pi(s)-\pi^\star(s)\|\le\varepsilon$
along its own rollout. Both policies start from $s_0$; let $\delta_t:=\|\hat s_t-s^\star_t\|$ be the
state deviation between the closed-loop rollout of $\hat\pi$ and the expert.

\begin{proposition}[Deviation bound]\label{prop:dev}
With $\delta_0=0$,
\[
\delta_H \;\le\; L_a\,\varepsilon\sum_{k=0}^{H-1}L^{k}
\;=\;\begin{cases} L_a\,\varepsilon\,\dfrac{L^{H}-1}{L-1}, & L\neq 1,\\[1.2ex] L_a\,\varepsilon\,H, & L=1.\end{cases}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\hat\pi(\hat s_t)=\pi^\star(\hat s_t)+e_t$ with $\|e_t\|\le\varepsilon$. Then
$\hat s_{t+1}-s^\star_{t+1}=f(\hat s_t,\pi^\star(\hat s_t)+e_t)-f(s^\star_t,\pi^\star(s^\star_t))$.
Adding and subtracting $f(\hat s_t,\pi^\star(\hat s_t))$ and applying the Lipschitz assumptions,
\[
\delta_{t+1}\le \|f(\hat s_t,\pi^\star(\hat s_t)+e_t)-f(\hat s_t,\pi^\star(\hat s_t))\|
+\|g(\hat s_t)-g(s^\star_t)\| \le L_a\varepsilon+L\,\delta_t .
\]
Unrolling $\delta_{t+1}\le L\delta_t+L_a\varepsilon$ from $\delta_0=0$ gives the geometric sum.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Three regimes.} The bound depends sharply on the closed-loop contraction $L$:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item $L<1$ (self-correcting): $\delta_H\le L_a\varepsilon/(1-L)$, bounded independent of horizon.
Errors do not accumulate, and validation loss --- an estimate of $\varepsilon$ --- is a faithful proxy.
\item $L=1$ (marginally stable): $\delta_H\le L_a\varepsilon H$, linear in horizon.
\item $L>1$ (expansive; contact-rich or unstable): $\delta_H$ grows like $\varepsilon\,L^{H}$, exponential
in horizon. A near-perfect validation loss (tiny $\varepsilon$) can still admit arbitrarily large deviation.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Two predictions our experiments test.} Validation loss estimates $\varepsilon$, the numerator
of Proposition~\ref{prop:dev}; deployment success is governed by whether $\delta_t$ stays inside the
task tolerance, the left-hand side. They agree only when $L\le 1$ and $H$ is small. Hence:
(1) the gap should grow with horizon and contact along the form $(L^H-1)/(L-1)$, which is exactly the
regime dependence H2 evaluates; and (2) a metric that estimates $\delta$ rather than $\varepsilon$
should predict success where validation loss fails. Open-loop replay distance (M5) measures accumulated
deviation under precisely this recurrence, and latent drift (M4) measures departure from the training
manifold, the event large $\delta_t$ triggers. This is the coherence hypothesis with a mechanism:
coherence metrics estimate the integral, local metrics estimate the integrand.

\paragraph{Two caveats, both in our favor.} First, validation loss estimates $\varepsilon$ on the
\emph{expert's} state distribution, whereas Proposition~\ref{prop:dev} uses the on-policy error, which is
typically larger under covariate shift; the bound is therefore optimistic about validation loss, so the
true gap is at least this severe. Second, translating the deviation bound into expected return via an
$L_r$-Lipschitz reward recovers the classical compounding-error scaling of behavior cloning
\citep{ross2010efficient,ross2011reduction}: $O(\varepsilon H^2)$ for non-self-correcting dynamics
against $O(\varepsilon H)$ when errors do not compound. Our point is not the asymptotic rate but its
calibration consequence --- the rate is precisely why an offline estimate of $\varepsilon$ is an
unreliable, regime-dependent surrogate for success.
