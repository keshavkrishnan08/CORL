The validation gap is, precisely, a \emph{selection} failure: of the checkpoints a run emits, the one
minimising validation loss need not maximise deployment success. We characterise exactly when a cheap
offline metric can rank checkpoints by closed-loop success, and frame it as the \textbf{query
complexity of policy selection} --- how much environment interaction a metric must spend to rank
correctly. The classical compounding bound enters only as a lemma; the contribution is a quantitative
predictiveness law with matched lower and upper bounds, resting on a single non-circular fact about
where the closed-loop gain lives.

\paragraph{Setup.} Dynamics $s_{t+1}=f(s_t,a_t)$ are $C^1$; $\pi^\star$ is the expert and
$g_\pi(s)=f(s,\pi(s))$ the closed-loop map of a policy $\pi$, with finite-horizon gain
$L(\pi)=\sup_{t<H}\|\partial g_\pi(s_t)\|$ along its rollout. A policy with per-step action error
$\varepsilon$ has deviation $\delta_t=\|\hat s_t-s^\star_t\|$ obeying $\delta_{t+1}\le L\,\delta_t+L_a\varepsilon$, hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dev}
\delta_H \;\le\; L_a\,\varepsilon\,A(L,H),\qquad
A(L,H):=\textstyle\sum_{k=0}^{H-1}L^k=\tfrac{L^H-1}{L-1}\ (L\!\neq\!1),\;\;H\ (L{=}1).
\end{equation}
Classical: the recurrence is discrete Gr\"onwall; the return form is
\citet{ross2010efficient,ross2011reduction}; the three regimes are \citet{spencer2021feedback};
exponential blow-up under unstable continuous dynamics is \citet{simchowitz2025pitfalls}; the
gain$\times$validation-loss factorisation of a \emph{single} policy's failure is \citet{tan2026gain}.
We claim none of \eqref{eq:dev}. Success is $\mathrm{Succ}(\pi)=\mathbf 1[\delta_H(\pi)<\tau]$. Under a
persistent error \eqref{eq:dev} is tight, $\delta_H=\varepsilon A(L,H)$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:bound}a,
$R^2{=}1.0$). Validation loss measures $\varepsilon$ on the expert distribution $d^\star$; deployment
measures $\delta_H$ on the policy's own occupancy.

\paragraph{The identifiability principle (why this is not circular).} The gain factors through the
environment: $\partial g_\pi(s)=\partial_s f + \partial_a f\,\partial\pi$. The term $\partial_a f$ ---
how the world amplifies an action perturbation --- is a property of $f$, evaluated at states the
\emph{policy itself} reaches, many of them off $d^\star$. We call a metric \emph{rollout-free} ($b{=}0$
environment transitions) if it is a functional of $\pi$ on the fixed distribution $d^\star$ (M1--M4,
M6, M8 --- including ensemble disagreement and policy-smoothness, which are still functions of $\pi$
alone); \emph{environment-querying} if it executes the policy (M5, M7). The key fact is not that such
metrics ``only see $\varepsilon$'' but that they \emph{cannot see $\partial f$ off $d^\star$}: no
functional of $\pi$ alone can identify $L(\pi)$, because two environments agreeing on $d^\star$ but
differing in $\partial_a f$ elsewhere give the same policy, the same $b{=}0$ score, and different
$\delta_H$. Everything below is a consequence.

\paragraph{Selection, and the decoupled regime.} A metric selects $\hat\pi_m=\arg\min_i m(\pi_i)$ with
regret $R(m)=\Pr[\exists i\,\mathrm{Succ}(\pi_i)]-\Pr[\mathrm{Succ}(\hat\pi_m)]$; predictiveness is
$\rho(m)=|\mathrm{Spearman}(m,\delta_H)|$. Let $\mathcal F^\star=\sigma(\pi\!\mid_{d^\star})$ be all that
$d^\star$ identifies. Two facts pin the problem. First, the per-step error $\varepsilon$ \emph{is} the
validation loss --- an $\mathcal F^\star$ functional --- so it is the most any $b{=}0$ metric can
exploit. Second, split the log-gain $\log L=\mu^\star+\xi$ into its $\mathcal F^\star$-measurable part
$\mu^\star$ and an orthogonal remainder $\xi$, the \emph{off-manifold gain} that only querying $f$ off
$d^\star$ reveals; write $q:=\mathrm{Var}\,\xi$. The hard, empirically typical case is the
\emph{decoupled} regime $\varepsilon\perp\log L$: how faithfully a policy imitates on-distribution says
nothing about how the world amplifies its drift off it.

\begin{theorem}[The off-manifold gain variance caps offline predictiveness]\label{thm:pred}
Model $(\log\varepsilon,\log L)$ as jointly Gaussian, so the rank statistic $\rho^2$ equals the Pearson
$R^2$ on $\log\delta_H$. Every $b{=}0$ metric is $\mathcal F^\star$-measurable, hence
$\rho^2(m)\le s(H)$, the variance share explained by the best $\mathcal F^\star$-predictor. In the
decoupled regime, using $\log\delta_H=\log\varepsilon+H\log L+O(1)$,
\[ s(H)=\frac{a+H^2 p}{\,a+H^2(p+q)\,},\qquad a:=\mathrm{Var}\log\varepsilon,\ \ p:=\mathrm{Var}\,\mu^\star,\ \ q:=\mathrm{Var}\,\xi, \]
strictly decreasing in $H$ whenever $q>0$ ($\partial_{(H^2)}s=-aq/[\,a+H^2(p+q)\,]^2<0$), with limit
$p/(p+q)$ and $\to0$ when the gain is unidentifiable on $d^\star$ ($p{=}0$). A single scalar, the
off-manifold gain variance $q$, sets the ceiling.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Measurability gives the cap. Decoupling annihilates the cross-covariances, so
$\mathrm{Var}\log\delta_H=a+H^2(p+q)$ with $\mathcal F^\star$-explained component $a+H^2p$ (from
$\log\varepsilon$ and $H\mu^\star$); their ratio is $s(H)$ and $\partial_{(H^2)}s<0$.
\end{proof}

This is the falsifiable core: \emph{offline predictiveness falls with horizon at a rate set by one
quantity, $q$}. Part~B tests the ordering across the short/medium/long ladder (H1--H3, a coarse
$3$-point test); the controlled sweep is the clean test (Fig.~\ref{fig:bound}b: $0.31$ vs.\ $0.84$).

\begin{theorem}[Zero-query impossibility]\label{thm:imposs}
Fix $L>1$ and $\zeta>0$. There exist $\pi_A,\pi_B$ with $|m(\pi_A)-m(\pi_B)|\le\zeta$ for \emph{every}
$b{=}0$ metric, yet $\mathrm{Succ}(\pi_A){=}1,\ \mathrm{Succ}(\pi_B){=}0$. Hence on any population whose
off-manifold gain $\xi$ is non-degenerate, every $b{=}0$ metric has $R(m)\ge R(\mathrm{random})-o(1)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Let $\pi_A,\pi_B$ agree with $\pi^\star$ on $\mathrm{supp}(d^\star)$ up to $\zeta$ (all $b{=}0$
functionals agree to $O(\zeta)$); since $L>1$ the shared on-manifold error drives the loop off
$d^\star$ in $\le H$ steps into a region where $f$ is contractive for $\pi_A$, expansive for $\pi_B$
(possible for one fixed $f$ under mild reachability: a contractive and an expansive region are both
reachable off $d^\star$). The label is then set by $\xi$, to which $b{=}0$ metrics are blind, so ranking
is independent of $\mathrm{Succ}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[One trajectory is necessary and sufficient]\label{thm:achieve}
A single open-loop replay (one trajectory, $H$ transitions) estimates $\delta_H$ to error $\gamma$ and
attains $R\le\Pr[\exists i:|\delta_H(\pi_i)-\tau|\le\gamma]\!\to\!0$ as $\gamma\!\to\!0$, in every
regime. Conversely (Thm~\ref{thm:imposs}), when $\xi$ is non-degenerate no $b{=}0$ metric attains
$o(1)$ regret. So in the expansive regime the environment budget for oracle selection drops
discontinuously from \emph{unattainable with zero trajectories} to \emph{achieved with one} ---
and a factor $N$ cheaper than the $N$-rollout closed-loop evaluation it replaces.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Outside the $\gamma$-band, $\arg\min_i m_{\mathrm{rep}}$ shares the success label of
$\arg\min_i\delta_H$, and the minimum-deviation checkpoint succeeds iff any does; regret is incurred
only within the band. Necessity is Thm~\ref{thm:imposs}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[The horizon wall is a phase transition]\label{cor:wall}
For $L>1$ a fixed $\varepsilon$ first breaches tolerance at
$H^\star=\log\!\big(1+\tfrac{\tau}{\varepsilon}(L-1)\big)/\log L$: $\delta_H$ is linear ($\varepsilon H$)
below and exponential ($\varepsilon L^H$) above, the crossover sharpening as $L\!\to\!1^+$. By
Thm~\ref{thm:pred}, offline predictiveness is already eroding before $H^\star$ and is uninformative
beyond it.
\end{corollary}
Predicted $H^\star$ matches the empirical collapse to within one step (Fig.~\ref{fig:bound}d: $12.8$
vs.\ $13$).

\begin{corollary}[Why one trajectory escapes the wall]\label{cor:m5}
If the closed loop has a contractive feedback margin off the replay path (so replaying expert actions
open-loop deviates at least as much as the closed loop), open-loop replay deviation upper-bounds
$\delta_H$; then $m_{\mathrm{rep}}$ is a conservative consistent estimator and satisfies
Thm~\ref{thm:achieve}. This is the assumption under which M5 ranks where validation loss cannot; we
test it directly rather than assume it (Part~B).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}[The controlling quantity is measurable]\label{prop:measurable}
The finite-time gain estimated from $\varepsilon$-perturbed rollouts, $\hat L$ (the Lyapunov estimate
of the Method) --- the mean log-divergence rate of paired rollouts whose initial states differ by
$\varepsilon$ --- converges to the finite-horizon gain $L$ in \eqref{eq:dev} as the perturbation
shrinks, so it is a consistent estimator. The theory's only free quantity is thus read off per task,
and Thm~\ref{thm:pred} predicts the ordering of validation-loss predictiveness from the measured
ordering of $\hat L$ --- a prediction Part~B can refute.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{What is new, against close prior work.} \citet{tan2026gain} factorise a \emph{single}
policy's failure into gain $\times$ validation loss; we study \emph{ranking across a population}, where
the controlling object is not the gain but its \emph{variance} $q$, and prove a selection impossibility
and a query-complexity dichotomy that do not follow from any per-policy bound. The cleanest way to place
the result is a \emph{selection--estimation gap}: ranking is normally far cheaper than off-policy
estimation of the return, but in the expansive regime that gap collapses --- both demand environment
access, and selection's price is exactly one trajectory, no fewer. The four results form a closed
characterisation: a decay law governed by $q$ (Thm~\ref{thm:pred}), matched bounds pinning ``can rank''
to a single bit of environment access (Thms~\ref{thm:imposs},~\ref{thm:achieve}), a sharp $H^\star$
(Cor.~\ref{cor:wall}), and a controlling gain that is measured, not assumed (Prop.~\ref{prop:measurable}).
The practical reading is blunt: past the wall no offline cleverness recovers the ranking, and one
environment query already does.
