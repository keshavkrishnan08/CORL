We register the entire analysis before collecting a single rollout. The code that produces
every quantity below is committed and hashed before training begins; nothing here can be
chosen after seeing the data. This section states what each test computes, the decision rule
that was fixed in advance, and what each outcome would establish.

\paragraph{H1 --- does the gap exist?} For every run we take the checkpoint with the lowest
validation L1 and the checkpoint with the highest measured success rate, and record the
difference in success: the gap. A one-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test across all \NRuns{}
runs asks whether the median gap exceeds zero. We call H1 supported only if the median clears
ten percentage points at the Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha = 0.0125$, and we report a
bias-corrected bootstrap interval on the median. Figure~\ref{fig:headline} plots the
validation-selected against the rollout-best success rate; every point that sits above the
diagonal is performance that validation loss left on the table. \emph{Pre-registered slot:
median gap \HoneGap{}~pp, $p = \HoneP{}$ (\HoneSupported{}).}

\paragraph{H2 --- does the gap depend on regime?} We fit a linear mixed-effects model of the
per-run gap on horizon and complexity, with a per-seed random intercept and architecture as a
fixed effect, and test the regime terms by likelihood ratio against an architecture-only null.
The pre-registered prediction is directional: long-horizon and contact-rich tasks should show
larger gaps, because compounding has more steps to act on. \emph{Slot: $p = \HtwoP{}$
(\HtwoSupported{}).}

\paragraph{H3 --- can an offline metric beat validation loss?} For each run we compute the
rank correlation between each of the eight offline metrics and success across that run's
checkpoints, sign-oriented so positive always means predictive. A paired Wilcoxon test
compares every alternative metric against validation L1, with an inner Bonferroni across the
seven comparisons. H3 is supported if any metric beats validation L1 at the corrected
threshold. \emph{Slot: strongest metric \HthreeBest{} (mean signed Spearman
\HthreeBestSpearman{}); \HthreeSupported{}.}

\paragraph{H4 --- does a composite generalise across tasks?} A ridge predictor with
cross-validated regularisation is fit on the six training tasks (pooling architectures and
seeds) and evaluated on the two held-out tasks, locked before training. H4 is supported if its
held-out RMSE is at least twenty percent below a validation-L1-only baseline; a
leave-one-task-out sweep reports sensitivity to the split. \emph{Slot: composite RMSE
\HfourCompRMSE{} vs baseline \HfourBaseRMSE{} (\HfourRel{}\% change; \HfourSupported{}).}

\paragraph{The coherence hypothesis (secondary).} The eight metrics split into signals of
\emph{local accuracy} (validation L1, delta-MSE, action confidence) and signals of
\emph{global coherence} (latent drift from the training manifold, open-loop replay fidelity,
trajectory jerk). If the gap is born of compounding, the coherence group should track success
better than the local group. We test the per-run difference in mean signed correlation with a
one-sided Wilcoxon. This is the mechanistic claim behind the phenomenon, reported as
exploratory rather than confirmatory.

\paragraph{Selection utility (secondary).} Rank correlation is not the same as usefulness. For
each metric we simulate the policy that selecting checkpoints by that metric would have
deployed, and report its expected and worst-decile success across runs. This answers the
question a practitioner actually asks --- which signal, used as the selection rule, yields the
best deployed policy --- rather than which signal merely correlates.

\paragraph{The study cannot return an uninterpretable null.} The four hypotheses partition
into distinct, citable conclusions (Table~\ref{tab:outcome}). A well-powered failure of H1
would itself be a finding: validation loss is adequate, and the field's alarm is miscalibrated.
With \NRuns{} runs the protocol has power above $0.95$ to detect a ten-point gap, so a null is
evidence, not absence of it.

\paragraph{External validation against real-robot outcomes (planned).} To test whether the
conclusions survive contact with hardware we never run, we compute the architecture-agnostic
offline metrics on the public generalist policies in SIMPLER's comparison set and ask whether
they predict the published \emph{real-world} success ranking better than validation MSE, whose
real-success correlation SIMPLER reports at Pearson $r = 0.308$. This validates the
cross-policy use of the metrics without new hardware; we are explicit that it does not test the
within-run checkpoint gap, and we report it as corroboration.
